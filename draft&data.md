1 Introduction

1.1 Background
Breast cancer remains one of the most prevalent and deadly cancers affecting women globally, accounting for a significant proportion of cancer-related morbidity and mortality. Early and accurate detection of breast tumors is critical for improving patient prognosis, enabling timely intervention, and reducing treatment costs. Among various imaging modalities, ultrasound imaging has emerged as a vital tool due to several inherent advantages. It offers real-time imaging capabilities, is relatively low-cost compared to modalities like MRI or mammography, is portable, and does not expose patients to ionizing radiation. These features make ultrasound particularly valuable for screening in populations with dense breast tissue, where mammography sensitivity is reduced, and in resource-limited clinical environments where access to advanced imaging is constrained.

Despite these benefits, ultrasound imaging presents unique challenges that complicate accurate diagnosis. The images are often degraded by speckle noise, which reduces contrast and obscures lesion boundaries. Additionally, the quality and interpretability of ultrasound images depend heavily on the operator’s skill and experience, introducing variability in diagnostic outcomes. To address these limitations, computer-aided diagnosis (CAD) systems leveraging deep learning have been developed. These systems aim to assist radiologists by automating lesion detection, classification, and segmentation tasks, thereby enhancing diagnostic consistency and accuracy. However, most existing CAD systems are designed for static ultrasound images, neglecting the dynamic nature of clinical ultrasound examinations that involve continuous video streams. These video sequences contain rich temporal and spatial information such as lesion persistence, boundary stability, and motion patterns across frames, which can provide additional diagnostic cues.

Incorporating temporal context into tumor detection holds promise for improving detection accuracy and reducing false positives by leveraging information continuity over time. However, existing spatial-temporal deep learning approaches, particularly those based on 3D convolutional neural networks, tend to be computationally intensive. This complexity poses significant barriers to deploying such models in clinical settings where low-latency inference and efficient resource utilization are essential. Therefore, there is a pressing need to develop lightweight spatial-temporal models that balance diagnostic performance with computational efficiency to enable real-time clinical applicability.

1.2 Problem

1.2.1 Original problem and definition
Current breast ultrasound CAD systems predominantly focus on analyzing individual frames extracted from ultrasound videos, thereby ignoring the temporal relationships between frames. This frame-based approach can lead to inconsistent tumor detections, increased false positive rates, and reduced diagnostic reliability. While spatial-temporal deep learning models have been proposed to capture temporal dependencies and improve detection stability, they often rely on complex architectures such as 3D CNNs or transformer-based models that demand substantial computational resources. These requirements limit their feasibility for deployment in clinical workflows, particularly in settings with constrained hardware capabilities or where real-time inference is critical. Consequently, the challenge lies in developing models that can effectively integrate temporal information to enhance tumor detection accuracy without compromising inference speed or resource efficiency.

1.2.2 Scientific and engineering issues
From a scientific perspective, the key challenge is to design spatial-temporal deep learning frameworks that can extract and integrate temporal features from ultrasound video sequences in a manner that improves detection robustness and accuracy. This involves selecting or developing temporal modeling modules that capture relevant temporal dynamics such as lesion motion and persistence while avoiding overfitting or excessive model complexity. From an engineering standpoint, the focus is on optimizing these models for low-latency inference. This includes architectural design choices to reduce computational overhead, such as using lightweight backbones, efficient temporal modules (e.g., ConvLSTM or attention mechanisms), and model compression techniques like pruning and quantization. Achieving this balance is critical to enabling practical deployment in clinical environments where computational resources and response times are limited.

1.3 Purpose
The primary purpose of this project is to develop and validate a lightweight deep learning framework that integrates spatial-temporal modeling for breast ultrasound video tumor detection. The framework aims to achieve high diagnostic accuracy while ensuring low-latency inference suitable for real-time clinical workflows. By combining advances in spatial-temporal deep learning architectures with model optimization techniques, this project seeks to bridge the gap between methodological innovation and practical clinical deployment. Ultimately, the goal is to contribute a prototype system that demonstrates the feasibility and benefits of efficient temporal modeling in breast ultrasound video analysis, providing a foundation for future translational AI research in medical imaging.

1.4 Goals  

- Implement a baseline tumor detection model that operates on individual frames extracted from breast ultrasound videos to establish reference performance metrics.  
- Design and develop spatial-temporal architectures that incorporate temporal modeling modules such as ConvLSTM, SlowFast, or transformer-based attention mechanisms to capture temporal dependencies across video frames.  
- Optimize the proposed models to achieve inference speeds of at least 15 frames per second, aligning with low-latency clinical requirements, without sacrificing detection accuracy.  
- Conduct comprehensive experimental evaluations including ablation studies, performance benchmarking, and computational complexity analysis to assess the trade-offs between accuracy and efficiency.  
- Develop a prototype system that integrates the optimized spatial-temporal model for real-time tumor detection on ultrasound video streams, demonstrating practical applicability in clinical workflows.

1.5 Research Methodology
The research methodology encompasses several interconnected phases. Initially, publicly available breast ultrasound video datasets will be collected and systematically preprocessed to ensure data quality and annotation consistency. Baseline detection models focusing on static frame analysis will be implemented using established convolutional neural network architectures. Building upon this, spatial-temporal models incorporating temporal modules such as ConvLSTM, SlowFast networks, or transformer-based attention will be developed to exploit temporal context in video sequences. Transfer learning techniques will be employed to leverage pre-trained weights, accelerating convergence and improving performance. Model optimization strategies including pruning and quantization will be applied to reduce computational complexity and enable low-latency inference. Experimental evaluations will involve quantitative metrics such as mean Average Precision (mAP), AUC-ROC, FROC, sensitivity, specificity, and frames per second (FPS) to comprehensively assess diagnostic performance and efficiency. Ablation studies will explore the impact of different architectural components and optimization methods. Finally, a prototype system will be developed to validate the model’s feasibility for real-time clinical deployment.

1.6 Delimitations
This project is specifically focused on tumor detection within breast ultrasound video sequences and does not extend to related tasks such as clinical diagnosis, detailed lesion segmentation, or multi-modal data fusion involving other imaging modalities. The evaluation will be limited to publicly available datasets and will not include clinical trials or direct integration with hospital information systems. Deployment efforts will culminate in a prototype demonstration rather than a fully integrated clinical solution. Regulatory, certification, and ethical approval processes for clinical use are outside the scope of this project. Additionally, the project will assume access to suitable computational resources but will prioritize model efficiency to facilitate potential deployment in resource-constrained environments.

1.7 Structure of the thesis
The thesis is organized into several key chapters. The Introduction and Background chapters provide foundational context, including the clinical importance of breast ultrasound imaging, challenges in tumor detection, and an overview of relevant deep learning technologies. The Literature Review synthesizes current research on spatial-temporal deep learning models, medical ultrasound image analysis, video-based medical image analysis, and efficient neural network design. The Research Methodology chapter details dataset selection, model architectures, training procedures, and evaluation metrics. The Experimental Results and Discussion chapter presents empirical findings, ablation studies, and analysis of computational efficiency. Finally, the Conclusion and Future Work chapter summarizes key contributions, discusses limitations, and outlines directions for further research and clinical translation.

2 Background

2.1 Major background area 1: Spatial-Temporal Deep Learning Models for Video Analysis
Spatial-temporal deep learning models extend traditional image analysis by incorporating temporal dynamics inherent in video data. These models simultaneously capture spatial features within individual frames and temporal dependencies across sequences, enabling richer representations that can improve performance on video-related tasks. Prominent architectures include 3D convolutional neural networks (3D CNNs), which apply convolutions across spatial and temporal dimensions; SlowFast networks, which process video at multiple temporal resolutions to balance efficiency and accuracy; ConvLSTM models, which integrate convolutional operations with recurrent units to model spatiotemporal sequences; and attention-based or transformer-based video models that leverage self-attention mechanisms to capture long-range dependencies. These architectures have demonstrated success in diverse applications such as action recognition, video classification, and object detection. Their potential for medical ultrasound video analysis lies in the ability to model lesion persistence, boundary stability, and motion patterns that are diagnostically relevant.

2.1.1 Subarea 1.1: 3D Convolutional Neural Networks
3D CNNs extend 2D convolutional filters by adding a temporal dimension, enabling simultaneous extraction of spatial and temporal features. This approach effectively captures motion and temporal context but often entails high computational complexity and memory usage. These demands can hinder real-time deployment, especially on hardware with limited resources.

2.1.2 Subarea 1.2: SlowFast Networks
SlowFast networks process video data through two parallel pathways: a slow pathway that operates at low frame rates to capture detailed spatial semantics, and a fast pathway that processes high frame rate inputs to capture motion dynamics. This dual-path design achieves a balance between accuracy and computational efficiency, making it promising for medical video analysis where both spatial detail and temporal dynamics are important.

2.1.3 Subarea 1.3: ConvLSTM Models
ConvLSTM networks combine convolutional layers with Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) units to model spatiotemporal dependencies. By applying recurrent units to convolutional feature maps, ConvLSTMs capture temporal sequences while preserving spatial structure. This architecture offers a compromise between modeling capacity and computational cost, suitable for sequential medical imaging data.

2.2 Major background area 2: Deep Learning for Medical Ultrasound Image Analysis
Deep learning has been extensively applied to static ultrasound image analysis tasks including tumor detection, classification, and segmentation. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have demonstrated strong diagnostic performance by learning hierarchical feature representations directly from image data. Many CAD systems utilize CNN-based architectures to assist radiologists in identifying breast lesions with improved accuracy and consistency. However, these methods typically analyze frames independently, failing to leverage temporal information available in ultrasound videos. This limitation may result in unstable detections and higher false positive rates.

2.3 Related work area: Video-Based Medical Image Analysis
A growing subset of research focuses on exploiting temporal information in medical imaging videos, including ultrasound. Studies have shown that incorporating temporal context can enhance detection stability, reduce false positives, and capture dynamic patterns relevant for diagnosis. These approaches often employ spatial-temporal deep learning architectures adapted from computer vision. Despite promising results, many existing methods rely on computationally intensive models that challenge low-latency deployment in clinical workflows. There remains a gap in developing efficient spatial-temporal models tailored for breast ultrasound video tumor detection that meet real-time inference requirements.

2.3.1 Major related work 1: Domain-Knowledge-Based Video Modeling
Some studies integrate clinical domain knowledge with deep learning to inform temporal modeling in ultrasound videos. By encoding relevant temporal patterns such as lesion persistence and motion characteristics, these approaches improve diagnostic outcomes and model interpretability.

2.3.2 Major related work 2: Efficiency-Oriented Medical Segmentation Models
Lightweight architectures like EdgeMedNet demonstrate that efficient network design and model compression techniques can maintain segmentation accuracy while significantly reducing computational cost. These insights are valuable for designing tumor detection models that balance performance with efficiency.

2.3.3 Minor related work 1: Frame-Based CAD Systems
Traditional CAD systems focusing on static frames provide a baseline for detection performance but lack temporal modeling, leading to potential inconsistencies in video analysis.

2.3.4 Minor related work 2: General Video Action Recognition Models
General video recognition architectures offer transferable design principles for temporal modeling. However, these models require adaptation to address the unique characteristics and constraints of medical ultrasound imaging.

2.4 Summary
While deep learning for static ultrasound image analysis is well-established, the integration of spatial-temporal modeling for breast ultrasound video tumor detection remains underexplored. Particularly, balancing detection accuracy with low-latency inference constraints is a critical challenge. This project aims to address this gap by investigating efficient spatial-temporal architectures that leverage temporal context to improve tumor detection robustness and clinical feasibility.
